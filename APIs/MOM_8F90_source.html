39 !* This program (MOM) simulates the ocean by numerically solving *
40 !* the hydrostatic primitive equations in generalized Lagrangian *
41 !* vertical coordinates, typically tracking stretched pressure (p*) *
42 !* surfaces or following isopycnals in the ocean's interior, and *
43 !* general orthogonal horizontal coordinates. Unlike earlier versions *
44 !* of MOM, in MOM6 these equations are horizontally discretized on an *
45 !* Arakawa C-grid. (It remains to be seen whether a B-grid dynamic *
46 !* core will be revived in MOM6 at a later date; for now applications *
47 !* requiring a B-grid discretization should use MOM5.1.) MOM6 offers *
48 !* a range of options for the physical parameterizations, from those *
49 !* most appropriate to highly idealized models for geophysical fluid *
50 !* dynamics studies to a rich suite of processes appropriate for *
51 !* realistic ocean simulations. The thermodynamic options typically *
52 !* use conservative temperature and preformed salinity as conservative*
53 !* state variables and a full nonlinear equation of state, but there *
54 !* are also idealized adiabatic configurations of the model that use *
55 !* fixed density layers. Version 6.0 of MOM continues in the long *
56 !* tradition of a commitment to climate-quality ocean simulations *
57 !* embodied in previous versions of MOM, even as it draws extensively *
58 !* on the lessons learned in the development of the Generalized Ocean *
59 !* Layered Dynamics (GOLD) ocean model, which was also primarily *
60 !* developed at NOAA/GFDL. MOM has also benefited tremendously from *
61 !* the FMS infrastructure, which it utilizes and shares with other *
62 !* component models developed at GFDL. *
63 !* *
64 !* When run is isopycnal-coordinate mode, the uppermost few layers *
65 !* are often used to describe a bulk mixed layer, including the *
66 !* effects of penetrating shortwave radiation. Either a split- *
67 !* explicit time stepping scheme or a non-split scheme may be used *
68 !* for the dynamics, while the time stepping may be split (and use *
69 !* different numbers of steps to cover the same interval) for the *
70 !* forcing, the thermodynamics, and for the dynamics. Most of the *
71 !* numerics are second order accurate in space. MOM can run with an *
72 !* absurdly thin minimum layer thickness. A variety of non-isopycnal *
73 !* vertical coordinate options are under development, but all exploit *
74 !* the advantages of a Lagrangian vertical coordinate, as discussed *
75 !* in detail by Adcroft and Hallberg (Ocean Modelling, 2006). *
76 !* *
77 !* Details of the numerics and physical parameterizations are *
78 !* provided in the appropriate source files. All of the available *
79 !* options are selected at run-time by parsing the input files, *
80 !* usually MOM_input and MOM_override, and the options choices are *
81 !* then documented for each run in MOM_param_docs. *
82 !* *
83 !* MOM6 integrates the equations forward in time in three distinct *
84 !* phases. In one phase, the dynamic equations for the velocities *
85 !* and layer thicknesses are advanced, capturing the propagation of *
86 !* external and internal inertia-gravity waves, Rossby waves, and *
87 !* other strictly adiabatic processes, including lateral stresses, *
88 !* vertical viscosity and momentum forcing, and interface height *
89 !* diffusion (commonly called Gent-McWilliams diffusion in depth- *
90 !* coordinate models). In the second phase, all tracers are advected *
91 !* and diffused along the layers. The third phase applies diabatic *
92 !* processes, vertical mixing of water properties, and perhaps *
93 !* vertical remapping to cause the layers to track the desired *
94 !* vertical coordinate. *
95 !* *
96 !* The present file (MOM.F90) orchestrates the main time stepping *
97 !* loops. One time integration option for the dynamics uses a split *
98 !* explicit time stepping scheme to rapidly step the barotropic *
99 !* pressure and velocity fields. The barotropic velocities are *
100 !* averaged over the baroclinic time step before they are used to *
101 !* advect thickness and determine the baroclinic accelerations. As *
102 !* described in Hallberg and Adcroft (2009), a barotropic correction *
103 !* is applied to the time-mean layer velocities to ensure that the *
104 !* sum of the layer transports agrees with the time-mean barotropic *
105 !* transport, thereby ensuring that the estimates of the free surface *
106 !* from the sum of the layer thicknesses agrees with the final free *
107 !* surface height as calculated by the barotropic solver. The *
108 !* barotropic and baroclinic velocities are kept consistent by *
109 !* recalculating the barotropic velocities from the baroclinic *
110 !* transports each time step. This scheme is described in Hallberg, *
111 !* 1997, J. Comp. Phys. 135, 54-65 and in Hallberg and Adcroft, 2009, *
112 !* Ocean Modelling, 29, 15-26. *
113 !* *
114 !* The other time integration options use non-split time stepping *
115 !* schemes based on the 3-step third order Runge-Kutta scheme *
116 !* described in Matsuno, 1966, J. Met. Soc. Japan, 44, 85-88, or on *
117 !* a two-step quasi-2nd order Runge-Kutta scheme. These are much *
118 !* slower than the split time-stepping scheme, but they are useful *
119 !* for providing a more robust solution for debugging cases where the *
120 !* more complicated split time-stepping scheme may be giving suspect *
121 !* solutions. *
122 !* *
123 !* There are a range of closure options available. Horizontal *
124 !* velocities are subject to a combination of horizontal biharmonic *
125 !* and Laplacian friction (based on a stress tensor formalism) and a *
126 !* vertical Fickian viscosity (perhaps using the kinematic viscosity *
127 !* of water). The horizontal viscosities may be constant, spatially *
128 !* varying or may be dynamically calculated using Smagorinsky's *
129 !* approach. A diapycnal diffusion of density and thermodynamic *
130 !* quantities is also allowed, but not required, as is horizontal *
131 !* diffusion of interface heights (akin to the Gent-McWilliams *
132 !* closure of geopotential coordinate models). The diapycnal mixing *
133 !* may use a fixed diffusivity or it may use the shear Richardson *
134 !* number dependent closure, like that described in Jackson et al. *
135 !* (JPO, 2008). When there is diapycnal diffusion, it applies to *
136 !* momentum as well. As this is in addition to the vertical viscosity,*
137 !* the vertical Prandtl always exceeds 1. A refined bulk-mixed layer *
138 !* is often used to describe the planetary boundary layer in realistic*
139 !* ocean simulations. *
140 !* *
141 !* MOM has a number of noteworthy debugging capabilities. *
142 !* Excessively large velocities are truncated and MOM will stop *
143 !* itself after a number of such instances to keep the model from *
144 !* crashing altogether. This is useful in diagnosing failures, *
145 !* or (by accepting some truncations) it may be useful for getting *
146 !* the model past the adjustment from an ill-balanced initial *
147 !* condition. In addition, all of the accelerations in the columns *
148 !* with excessively large velocities may be directed to a text file. *
149 !* Parallelization errors may be diagnosed using the DEBUG option, *
150 !* which causes extensive checksums to be written out along with *
151 !* comments indicating where in the algorithm the sums originate and *
152 !* what variable is being summed. The point where these checksums *
153 !* differ between runs is usually a good indication of where in the *
154 !* code the problem lies. All of the test cases provided with MOM *
155 !* are routinely tested to ensure that they give bitwise identical *
156 !* results regardless of the domain decomposition, or whether they *
157 !* use static or dynamic memory allocation. *
158 !* *
159 !* About 115 other files of source code and 4 header files comprise *
160 !* the MOM code, although there are several hundred more files that *
161 !* make up the FMS infrastructure upon which MOM is built. Each of *
162 !* the MOM files contains comments documenting what it does, and *
163 !* most of the file names are fairly self-evident. In addition, all *
164 !* subroutines and data types are referenced via a module use, only *
165 !* statement, and the module names are consistent with the file names,*
166 !* so it is not too hard to find the source file for a subroutine. *
167 !* *
168 !* The typical MOM directory tree is as follows: *
169 !* ../MOM *
170 !* |-- config_src *
171 !* | |-- coupled_driver *
172 !* | |-- dynamic *
173 !* | `-- solo_driver *
174 !* |-- examples *
175 !* | |-- CM2G *
176 !* | |-- ... *
177 !* | `-- torus_advection_test *
178 !* `-- src *
179 !* |-- core *
180 !* |-- diagnostics *
181 !* |-- equation_of_state *
182 !* |-- framework *
183 !* |-- ice_shelf *
184 !* |-- initialization *
185 !* |-- parameterizations *
186 !* | |-- lateral *
187 !* | `-- vertical *
188 !* |-- tracer *
189 !* `-- user *
190 !* Rather than describing each file here, each directory's contents *
191 !* will be described to give a broad overview of the MOM code *
192 !* structure. *
193 !* *
194 !* The directories under config_src contain files that are used for *
195 !* configuring the code, for instance for coupled or ocean-only runs. *
196 !* Only one or two of these directories are used in compiling any, *
197 !* particular run. *
198 !* *
199 !* config_src/coupled_driver: *
200 !* The files here are used to couple MOM as a component in a larger *
201 !* run driven by the FMS coupler. This includes code that converts *
202 !* various forcing fields into the code structures and flux and unit*
203 !* conventions used by MOM, and converts the MOM surface fields *
204 !* back to the forms used by other FMS components. *
205 !* config_src/dynamic: *
206 !* The only file here is the version of MOM_memory.h that is used *
207 !* for dynamic memory configurations of MOM. *
208 !* config_src/solo_driver: *
209 !* The files here are include the _main driver that is used when *
210 !* MOM is configured as an ocean-only model, as well as the files *
211 !* that specify the surface forcing in this configuration. *
212 !* *
213 !* The directories under examples provide a large number of working *
214 !* configurations of MOM, along with reference solutions for several *
215 !* different compilers on GFDL's latest large computer. The versions *
216 !* of MOM_memory.h in these directories need not be used if dynamic *
217 !* memory allocation is desired, and the answers should be unchanged. *
218 !* *
219 !* The directories under src contain most of the MOM files. These *
220 !* files are used in every configuration using MOM. *
221 !* *
222 !* src/core: *
223 !* The files here constitute the MOM dynamic core. This directory *
224 !* also includes files with the types that describe the model's *
225 !* lateral grid and have defined types that are shared across *
226 !* various MOM modules to allow for more succinct and flexible *
227 !* subroutine argument lists. *
228 !* src/diagnostics: *
229 !* The files here calculate various diagnostics that are anciliary *
230 !* to the model itself. While most of these diagnostics do not *
231 !* directly affect the model's solution, there are some, like the *
232 !* calculation of the deformation radius, that are used in some *
233 !* of the process parameterizations. *
234 !* src/equation_of_state: *
235 !* These files describe the physical properties of sea-water, *
236 !* including both the equation of state and when it freezes. *
237 !* src/framework: *
238 !* These files provide infrastructure utilities for MOM. Many are *
239 !* simply wrappers for capabilities provided by FMS, although others*
240 !* provide capabilities (like the file_parser) that are unique to *
241 !* MOM. When MOM is adapted to use a different modeling *
242 !* infrastructure, most of the required changes are in this *
243 !* directory. *
244 !* src/initialization: *
245 !* These are the files that are used to initialize the MOM grid *
246 !* or provide the initial physical state for MOM. These files are *
247 !* not intended to be modified, but provide a means for calling *
248 !* user-specific initialization code like the examples in src/user. *
249 !* src/parameterizations/lateral: *
250 !* These files implement a number of quasi-lateral (along-layer) *
251 !* process parameterizations, including lateral viscosities, *
252 !* parameterizations of eddy effects, and the calculation of tidal *
253 !* forcing. *
254 !* src/parameterizations/vertical: *
255 !* These files implement a number of vertical mixing or diabatic *
256 !* processes, including the effects of vertical viscosity and *
257 !* code to parameterize the planetary boundary layer. There is a *
258 !* separate driver that orchestrates this portion of the algorithm, *
259 !* and there is a diversity of parameterizations to be found here. *
260 !* src/tracer: *
261 !* These files handle the lateral transport and diffusion of *
262 !* tracers, or are the code to implement various passive tracer *
263 !* packages. Additional tracer packages are readily accomodated. *
264 !* src/user: *
265 !* These are either stub routines that a user could use to change *
266 !* the model's initial conditions or forcing, or are examples that *
267 !* implement specific test cases. These files can easily be hand *
268 !* edited to create new analytically specified configurations. *
269 !* *
270 !* *
271 !* Most simulations can be set up by modifying only the files *
272 !* MOM_input, and possibly one or two of the files in src/user. *
273 !* In addition, the diag_table (MOM_diag_table) will commonly be *
274 !* modified to tailor the output to the needs of the question at *
275 !* hand. The FMS utility mkmf works with a file called path_names *
276 !* to build an appropriate makefile, and path_names should be edited *
277 !* to reflect the actual location of the desired source code. *
278 !* *
279 !* *
280 !* There are 3 publicly visible subroutines in this file (MOM.F90). *
281 !* step_MOM steps MOM over a specified interval of time. *
282 !* MOM_initialize calls initialize and does other initialization *
283 !* that does not warrant user modification. *
284 !* calculate_surface_state determines the surface (mixed layer) *
285 !* properties of the current model state and packages pointers *
286 !* to these fields into an exported structure. *
287 !* *
288 !* The remaining subroutines in this file (src/core/MOM.F90) are: *
289 !* find_total_transport determines the barotropic mass transport. *
290 !* register_diags registers many diagnostic fields for the dynamic *
291 !* solver, or of the main model variables. *
292 !* MOM_timing_init initializes various CPU time clocks. *
293 !* write_static_fields writes out various time-invariant fields. *
294 !* set_restart_fields is used to specify those fields that are *
295 !* written to and read from the restart file. *
296 !* smooth_SSH applies filtering of the sea-surface height field that *
297 !* is reported back to the calling routine; it is rarely used. *
298 !* *
299 !* Macros written all in capital letters are defined in MOM_memory.h. *
300 !* *
301 !* A small fragment of the grid is shown below: *
302 !* *
303 !* j+1 x ^ x ^ x At x: q, CoriolisBu *
304 !* j+1 > o > o > At ^: v, PFv, CAv, vh, diffv, tauy, vbt, vhtr *
305 !* j x ^ x ^ x At >: u, PFu, CAu, uh, diffu, taux, ubt, uhtr *
306 !* j > o > o > At o: h, bathyT, eta, T, S, tr *
307 !* j-1 x ^ x ^ x *
308 !* i-1 i i+1 *
309 !* i i+1 *
310 !* *
311 !* The boundaries always run through q grid points (x). *
312 !* *
